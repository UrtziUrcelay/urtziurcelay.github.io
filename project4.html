Hierarchical pathfinding

In almost every game there are moving actors, enemies, players, NPCâ€¦. All those actors must follow a path, sometimes the path has been previously scripted, but most of the times, due to the environmental changes of the map during runtime, the precomputed path has been obstructed or is no longer accessible. Here is when pathfinding comes into scene.

When we refer to pathfinding algorithms in games, we are mostly referring to A* search algorithm or any of its specific implementations. When applied to small maps, A* algorithm can be fast and memory consuming, but when applied to large maps with multiple moving actors, the pathfinding time becomes a limiting factor.

Most of the times we the actor will not reach the end of the path, due to a strategy change, the actor has been killed or whatever reason. It this case, most of the path calculus will be wasted, and we cannot afford to waste those valuable processor cycles. To avoid this, we will compute the path piece by piece, instead of the full path in one call. We will abstract the map into a group of smaller linked clusters or regions. We will compute a pre-path among the linked clusters, with almost no effort, since the search space will be much smaller, then we will compute the specific path inside each cluster when we are close to it. This way no calculus is wasted, and makes the algorithm also suitable for dynamic maps. We will get our actor moving as soon as we click, and during his way to the objective, we could easily have recomputed the abstract path if needed and the specific path inside the new clusters.

This hierarchy can have any number of levels, making it easily scalable to even larger problems or bigger search spaces. The more levels our hierarchy has, the more expensive in memory our algorithm will be in pursuit of smaller search effort.
